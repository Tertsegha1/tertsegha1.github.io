Stage 05 · The Concept

What is Training & Tuning?

Training & Tuning
The iterative process of fitting the model to data and optimising its configuration until performance meets the objective. Training adjusts internal parameters through repeated exposure. Tuning adjusts the external settings — hyperparameters — that govern how training runs. Neither alone is sufficient.

The selected model begins with no knowledge. Its parameters are initialised at random. Training is the process by which it encounters data, makes predictions, measures how wrong it is, and adjusts its internal weights to be less wrong on the next pass. Repeat this across thousands — sometimes millions — of iterations, and the model gradually converges on a configuration that captures the underlying pattern.

But training parameters are only part of the story. The hyperparameters — the settings that control how training runs — determine whether that convergence happens efficiently, at all, or not without diverging. These are not learned from data. They are designed by the engineer.

"A model that trains but never measures its loss is just practising. A model that measures loss but never adjusts is just observing. Growth only happens in the loop that does both — and does them repeatedly."

In AutoML, hyperparameter tuning is automated: the system searches systematically for the combination of settings — learning rate, batch size, depth, dropout — that produces the best validation performance. It is the equivalent of not just practising, but continuously asking: are these conditions for my practice optimal?

The training loop — four phases, none optional

Every effective training cycle runs all four phases. Skipping any one of them does not simplify the process — it breaks it.

⚡
Forward Pass
The model makes a prediction on the input data — committing to an output before seeing the answer.
The attempt. You commit to an approach and execute it, even without certainty about the outcome. No pass, no learning.
→
📉
Loss Calculation
The gap between the prediction and the ground truth is measured precisely. Loss is not failure — it is information.
Honest measurement. Not "how did I feel?" but "how far was my output from what the situation actually needed?" Precise signal, not vague sense.
→
🔄
Backpropagation
The loss signal travels back through the model, assigning responsibility to each parameter — identifying exactly what needs to change.
Root cause attribution. Not just "that didn't work" — but specifically which assumption, habit, or behaviour contributed to the gap. The harder and more important step.
→
🎯
Parameter Update
Each parameter adjusts by a small amount in the direction that reduces loss — governed by the learning rate.
The adjustment. A specific, deliberate change to behaviour, approach, or assumption — sized appropriately. Too large and the model destabilises. Too small and nothing shifts.
The loop is not optional. You can attempt without measuring. You can measure without adjusting. You can adjust without tracing the root cause. But only the complete loop produces a learning system. Partial loops produce the sensation of effort without the reality of improvement.

The learning rate — your pace of change

Of all the hyperparameters, the learning rate has the single greatest impact on whether training succeeds. It governs the size of each adjustment — how fast the model attempts to move toward the correct answer.

Too Low
Imperceptible progress
Adjustments are so small that convergence takes an impractical number of iterations — or stalls entirely in a shallow minimum.
Making changes so incremental that momentum never builds and nothing measurably shifts over weeks or months. The model is technically training but practically stagnant.
Optimal
Steady convergence
Each iteration makes meaningful progress without destabilising the model. Loss decreases consistently; generalisation improves.
Adjustments are sized to produce visible progress without destroying what is already working. One significant change per week, fully integrated before the next one arrives.
Too High
Chaotic oscillation
Adjustments overshoot the target — the model bounces around the loss landscape without converging, or diverges entirely.
Trying to change everything at once. Each adjustment undoes the last. The model is in constant motion but never settles. Burnout is often a learning rate problem.
The Human Parallel

Most people are training — but the loop is broken

Almost everyone is attempting. They are showing up, putting in effort, accumulating experience. The forward pass runs constantly. The problem is almost never effort — it is everything that comes after the attempt.

The loss is rarely calculated precisely. "That didn't go well" is not a loss function — it is a sentiment. A loss function asks: specifically, in what way did the output diverge from what was needed? By how much? Under what conditions does this divergence appear reliably?

Backpropagation — tracing the loss back to its source — is the step almost everyone skips. It requires sitting with discomfort long enough to identify the specific assumption or behaviour that produced the gap. It is easier to restart training, change the data, or increase effort. None of those address the parameter that actually caused the loss.

And even when a root cause is identified, the parameter update is often sized wrong. Either nothing changes — the insight is acknowledged and filed, but no behaviour shifts. Or everything changes at once — a complete overhaul that produces instability, disrupts what was already working, and creates new loss before the first adjustment has had time to settle.

This is why consistent effort without consistent growth is so common. The forward pass is strong. The rest of the loop is absent.

Hyperparameters — the conditions of your practice

In machine learning, the hyperparameters are set before training begins. They do not change during the run — but they determine the quality of every iteration. In your life, these are the conditions under which you practise:

  • Batch size: How much experience you process per learning cycle. Too large and you learn general trends without precision. Too small and you are reactive to noise. Most people never define their batch — they process whatever arrives.
  • Epochs: How many complete passes through your training data you run before evaluating. Single-pass learning — experiencing something once and moving on — is the weakest form of training. Deliberate repetition is how patterns consolidate.
  • Regularisation: The constraint that prevents overfitting to recent experience. Without it, a single vivid failure or success dominates the model. Regularisation keeps the learning general, not fixated on the last memorable event.
  • Early stopping: The discipline to halt training before it starts reinforcing poor patterns. Not every additional iteration improves the model. After a point, continued training on imperfect data embeds the imperfections more deeply.
Tuning the hyperparameters of your practice — the time of day, the environment, the duration, the review cadence, the feedback source — matters as much as the practice itself. You can be doing the right thing in conditions that guarantee poor learning.
In Your Life · Every Day

Closing the loop — daily and weekly

AutoML runs training in epochs — multiple complete passes through the data, measuring performance after each one and adjusting the model before the next begins. A single pass is insufficient. The pattern only becomes learnable through repeated, structured exposure, with measurement between cycles.

Your daily practice works the same way. A single good day of effort does not train the model. What trains the model is the rhythm of attempt → measure → adjust → repeat, running consistently enough that each cycle builds on the last. The minimum viable training loop has a cadence. Without cadence, there are only isolated attempts — each starting from where the last one ended by accident, not by design.

The goal is not to train harder. The goal is to close the loop more completely — and to keep it running at a pace your system can actually sustain.

The training loop running in daily life

Each moment below is a phase of the training cycle. The question is whether all four phases are running — or whether training is stalling between them.

Deep work — the forward pass
Partial: attempt runs, but with no defined output or target — effort without a measurable objective against which loss can be calculated
Full loop: the attempt has a specific, measurable output defined before it begins — a draft, a decision, a prototype. The forward pass needs a target to generate a loss signal.

Partial loopFull loop
Evening review — calculating the loss
Partial: the day ends with a general sense of how it went — a feeling, not a measurement. Loss is vague. Backpropagation cannot run on vague.
Full loop: a precise, honest comparison between what the attempt produced and what the objective required. Not "that was okay" — but specifically where the gap was and under what conditions it appeared.
🔄

Partial loopFull loop
Backpropagation — finding what produced the gap
Partial: loss is noticed but not traced. "That didn't work" is filed without identifying which specific assumption, habit, or behaviour contributed to the divergence
Full loop: follow the loss signal backward. Which decision point produced it? Which assumption was wrong? Which habit triggered the deviation from target? Name the specific parameter — not the category, the specific one.
🎯
The adjust
Partial loopFull loop
One specific change — the parameter update
Partial: the insight is acknowledged, but nothing specifically changes before the next iteration — or everything changes at once, destabilising what was already working
Full loop: one specific, observable change to one identified parameter before the next attempt runs. Not a resolution, a behaviour. Small enough to integrate, meaningful enough to shift the loss.
📋
Weekly
Partial loopFull loop
Hyperparameter review — are the conditions of practice still optimal?
Partial: conditions of practice are never reviewed — same time, same environment, same duration, same feedback sources, regardless of whether they are still producing efficient learning
Full loop: once a week, tune the hyperparameters. Is the training time still optimal? Is the feedback source still accurate? Is the batch size right — or are you learning from too much or too little at once?
